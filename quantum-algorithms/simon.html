Simon's Algorithm

Exponential Speedup for Hidden Period Problems

Overview

Simon's algorithm, proposed by David Simon in 1994, was one of the first quantum algorithms to demonstrate an exponential speedup over classical algorithms. It solves the hidden subgroup problem for a specific case, inspiring later algorithms like Shor's.

The Problem

Given a function \( f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^n \) that is promised to have a hidden period \( s \in \{0,1\}^n \) such that:

$$ f(x) = f(y) \iff x \oplus y \in \{0^n, s\} $$

Find the hidden period \( s \) (where \( \oplus \) is bitwise XOR).

Quantum Circuit

Simon's algorithm uses \( 2n \) qubits: \( n \) for the input and \( n \) for the output. The circuit applies Hadamard gates, queries the oracle, and measures to extract linear constraints on \( s \).

üí° Circuit Components
  • Blue Gates (H): Hadamard gates create and analyze superposition
  • Purple Box (U_f): Oracle implementing the periodic function
  • Green Symbols: Measurement yields equations about hidden period s

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1 Initialize to Superposition

Start with \( 2n \) qubits in \( |0\rangle \) and apply Hadamard to the first \( n \) qubits:

$$ |\psi_0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} |x\rangle |0^n\rangle $$
2 Query the Oracle

Apply the oracle \( U_f \) to compute \( f(x) \):

$$ |\psi_1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}} \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} |x\rangle |f(x)\rangle $$

The oracle evaluates the function for all inputs simultaneously.

3 Measure Output Register

Measuring the second register collapses the state to pairs \( \{x, x \oplus s\} \):

$$ |\psi_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left( |x_0\rangle + |x_0 \oplus s\rangle \right) $$

Both \( x_0 \) and \( x_0 \oplus s \) map to the same \( f \) value.

4 Apply Hadamard Transform

Apply Hadamard gates to the input register:

$$ |\psi_3\rangle = \frac{1}{2^{n/2}} \sum_{y \in \{0,1\}^n} (-1)^{x_0 \cdot y} \left(1 + (-1)^{s \cdot y}\right) |y\rangle $$

The term \( (1 + (-1)^{s \cdot y}) \) is 2 when \( s \cdot y = 0 \) and 0 when \( s \cdot y = 1 \).

5 Measure and Get Constraint

Measurement yields \( y \) such that:

$$ s \cdot y = 0 \pmod{2} $$

This gives us one linear equation over \( \mathbb{F}_2 \) (binary field).

6 Repeat and Solve

Repeat steps 1-5 approximately \( n \) times to get \( n \) linearly independent equations:

$$ \begin{cases} y_1 \cdot s = 0 \\ y_2 \cdot s = 0 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \cdot s = 0 \end{cases} $$

Solve this system using Gaussian elimination to find \( s \).

Applications & Significance

Theoretical Impact

Inspired Shor's Algorithm

Simon's work on hidden periods directly influenced the development of Shor's factoring algorithm.

Quantum Complexity

Provided early evidence of exponential quantum speedup, establishing separation between BQP and BPP.

Cryptanalysis

Breaks certain symmetric cryptographic constructions based on periodicity.

Hidden Subgroup

Solves a special case of the Hidden Subgroup Problem, a central problem in quantum algorithms.

Why It Matters

  • First Exponential Speedup: One of the first algorithms to show exponential quantum advantage
  • Oracle Separation: Proves BQP ‚â† BPP in the black-box model
  • Algorithm Design: Techniques used in Simon's algorithm inspired many other quantum algorithms
  • Educational Value: Simpler than Shor's but demonstrates key quantum algorithmic principles
