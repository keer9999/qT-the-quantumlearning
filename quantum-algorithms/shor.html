Shor's Factoring Algorithm

Breaking RSA Encryption with Quantum Computing

Overview

Shor's algorithm, discovered by Peter Shor in 1994, is a quantum algorithm for integer factorization. It can factor large numbers exponentially faster than the best known classical algorithms, posing a significant threat to widely-used RSA encryption.

The Problem

Given a large composite number \( N \), find its prime factors \( p \) and \( q \) such that \( N = p \times q \).

Quantum Circuit

Shor's algorithm uses two quantum registers: an input register and an output register. The key quantum component is the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) used for period finding.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1 Classical Pre-Processing

Choose random \( a < N \) and check if \( \gcd(a, N)> 1 \). If so, we found a factor!

Otherwise, proceed to find the period \( r \) of \( f(x) = a^x \mod N \).

2 Initialize Quantum Registers

Prepare two registers in superposition:

$$ |\psi_0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{Q}} \sum_{x=0}^{Q-1} |x\rangle |0\rangle $$

where \( Q = 2^q \) and \( q \) is chosen such that \( N^2 \leq Q < 2N^2 \).

3 Modular Exponentiation

Compute \( f(x) = a^x \mod N \) in superposition:

$$ |\psi_1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{Q}} \sum_{x=0}^{Q-1} |x\rangle |a^x \mod N\rangle $$

This is the quantum part that evaluates the function for all \( x \) simultaneously.

4 Measure Output Register

Measuring the second register collapses the state to values with the same \( a^x \mod N \):

$$ |\psi_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{k}} \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} |x_0 + jr\rangle $$

where \( r \) is the period we're looking for.

5 Apply Quantum Fourier Transform

Apply inverse QFT to extract the period:

$$ QFT^\dagger |\psi_2\rangle \approx |Q/r\rangle $$

Measuring gives us multiples of \( Q/r \), from which we can deduce \( r \).

6 Classical Post-Processing

Use continued fractions to find \( r \) from the measurement. Then compute factors:

$$ p = \gcd(a^{r/2} - 1, N), \quad q = \gcd(a^{r/2} + 1, N) $$

If \( r \) is odd or \( a^{r/2} \equiv -1 \mod N \), repeat with different \( a \).

Why It Works: Period Finding

üîë Key Insight

Factoring reduces to period finding: finding \( r \) such that \( a^r \equiv 1 \mod N \).

If \( r \) is even, then \( a^r - 1 = (a^{r/2} - 1)(a^{r/2} + 1) \equiv 0 \mod N \).

Since \( N \) divides the product but (hopefully) doesn't divide either factor individually, \( \gcd(a^{r/2} \pm 1, N) \) gives us the prime factors!

Example: Factoring 15

Let \( N = 15 \) and \( a = 7 \):

  • \( 7^1 \mod 15 = 7 \)
  • \( 7^2 \mod 15 = 4 \)
  • \( 7^3 \mod 15 = 13 \)
  • \( 7^4 \mod 15 = 1 \) ‚Üí period \( r = 4 \)

Compute: \( \gcd(7^2 - 1, 15) = \gcd(48, 15) = 3 \) and \( \gcd(7^2 + 1, 15) = \gcd(50, 15) = 5 \)

Success! Factors are 3 and 5.

Applications & Impact

Cryptographic Impact

RSA Encryption

Breaks RSA by efficiently factoring the public modulus N into primes p and q.

Digital Signatures

Compromises RSA-based digital signature schemes used for authentication.

Post-Quantum Crypto

Motivated development of quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms.

Key Exchange

Threatens Diffie-Hellman and other discrete logarithm-based protocols.

Broader Significance

  • Quantum Fourier Transform: Introduced QFT, now fundamental to many quantum algorithms
  • Practical Motivation: Provided strong incentive for building quantum computers
  • Complexity Theory: Showed BQP ‚â† BPP (quantum computers can solve harder problems)
  • Security Migration: Driving transition to post-quantum cryptography (NIST standards)
